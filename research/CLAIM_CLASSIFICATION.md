# Claim classification

Status: research governance; not accepted OME canon.

## Purpose

OME uses six labels to distinguish prior knowledge, integration, conjecture and
normative choice. A label describes the present evidential status of a claim;
it is not a measure of the claim's importance and it may change as evidence
develops.

No concept enters the OME canon until its relationship to adjacent disciplines
has been documented.

## Classes

### Established

A recognised concept, finding or practice supported by authoritative standards
or a credible body of prior research with substantially the same meaning.

Required record:

- a precise definition;
- primary or authoritative citations;
- the field and terminology from which OME inherits it; and
- any material disagreement or limitation in that field.

OME must use the established term unless a different label has a documented,
substantive purpose. “Established” does not mean universally true or immune to
revision.

### Synthesis

A purposeful integration, extension or application of established concepts
whose combination has explanatory or practical value for OME.

Required record:

- the constituent established concepts and their sources;
- the scope and mechanism of the integration;
- the added value claimed for the combination; and
- the boundaries beyond which the synthesis should not be applied.

A synthesis must not imply that OME invented its constituents. Moving a
hypothesis to synthesis requires demonstrating that the integration is coherent
and distinct enough to evaluate, not merely assigning it a new name.

### Hypothesis

A precise, falsifiable claim for which OME does not yet have sufficient
validation.

Required record:

- the claim and unit of analysis;
- the predicted relationship or outcome;
- observable evidence that could support or refute it;
- known confounders and plausible alternative explanations; and
- current evidence and counter-evidence.

A hypothesis may become a supported part of a synthesis after replicated or
otherwise suitably strong evidence within its claimed scope. It becomes
established only when a credible external body of knowledge supports
substantially the same claim; OME cannot declare its own proposal established by
definition.

### Principle

A transparent normative commitment governing how OME should be designed or
practised. A principle states what ought to be done; it is not presented as a
natural law or empirical finding.

Required record:

- the value or risk the principle addresses;
- its expected consequences and trade-offs;
- circumstances in which it may conflict with another principle; and
- relevant empirical evidence, where available.

Evidence may strengthen or weaken the justification for a principle, but does
not convert a normative choice into an established empirical fact.

### Novel proposal

A newly proposed construct, model, method or term for which no close equivalent
has been identified and which has a clear purpose beyond rebranding.

Required record:

- a precise definition and intended use;
- a documented adjacent-disciplines search;
- comparison with the closest existing concepts;
- the explanatory or practical gap it addresses; and
- a validation plan and conditions for abandoning it.

Novelty is provisional until external review. A proposal normally enters the
research programme as a hypothesis or testable method; novelty alone is not a
reason for canonisation.

### Open question

A material unresolved issue for which the available definitions, theory or
evidence do not yet justify a claim.

Required record:

- the question and why it matters;
- what is already known;
- competing answers or sources of uncertainty; and
- the research or decision needed to resolve it.

An open question should not be converted into a confident term, metric or
principle merely to make the framework appear complete.

## Classification changes

Every change of classification should be reviewable. The change record must
identify:

1. the previous and proposed classifications;
2. new evidence, counter-evidence or analysis;
3. the adjacent disciplines checked;
4. the scope for which the new classification is claimed; and
5. the reviewer and date.

Classification is conservative: uncertainty is recorded rather than averaged
away. When a statement contains both empirical and normative content, split it
into separately classified claims.
