## E-007 — Turnover and Knowledge Loss

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** incomplete. The
[publisher record](https://doi.org/10.1111/joms.12096) identifies an accepted
manuscript and version of record, but access is restricted. The publisher
abstract and RePEc bibliographic record were inspected. This is not represented
as a completed full-text appraisal.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation, sample size and contextual direction
are supported by the accessible material. The study reports a survey of 150
service and manufacturing firms and compares the performance associations of
production-manager and production-worker turnover. The existing registration
was too strong in calling the study one “of turnover, knowledge loss and
performance relationships”: accessible material shows that knowledge loss is
the theorised mechanism, while turnover and firm performance are the reported
empirical relationship.

**Method:** provisionally, a cross-sectional multi-firm survey comparing service
and manufacturing contexts and testing whether organisational capital moderates
turnover–performance relationships. Measurement, sampling, response-rate and
model details require the full article.

**Provisional finding relevant to C-002:** production-worker turnover had a
stronger negative relationship with performance in service firms than in
manufacturing firms, and a stronger negative relationship than
production-manager turnover within service firms. Organisational capital
moderated the worker-turnover relationship in service firms. These findings
support contingency and structural insulation; they do not directly observe
knowledge becoming unavailable.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Inferred | Departing managers and workers are theorised to possess production knowledge, but prior reasoning is not identified in the accessible material. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Not directly measured | Turnover is the exposure and firm performance is the outcome; inability to retrieve or apply prior reasoning is not observed. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Not measured | No reconstruction activity or effort is reported in the accessible material. |

**Alternative explanations:** service dependence on human action, manufacturing
insulation of the technical core, role differences, organisational capital and
other turnover–performance mechanisms can explain heterogeneous effects without
demonstrating reconstruction.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** turnover is not equivalent to knowledge loss;
firm performance is not reconstruction work; the accessible material does not
permit appraisal of construct measures, temporal order, common-method risk or
model specification; and no reasoning artifact or retrieval event is observed.

**Research Lead direction:** provisionally `Contextual`. The study establishes
heterogeneity and a buffering mechanism adjacent to C-002, not the full claim.

## E-008 — When Does Employee Turnover Matter?

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** incomplete. The
[Oxford Academic record](https://doi.org/10.1093/jopart/muaf019) provides
publisher metadata and the complete abstract but requires purchase for the
article. This is not represented as a completed full-text appraisal.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation, population, date range, model family
and contextual direction are supported by the publisher abstract. The dataset
covers IT projects implemented by 94 United States federal agencies from fiscal
years 2015 through 2021. The paper uses fractional panel probit models. Details
of the unit of analysis, variable construction, panel structure, controls and
robustness tests require full-text review.

**Method:** provisionally, observational panel analysis of IT-project delay,
workforce turnover, centralised chief-information-officer authority and task
standardisation across federal agencies.

**Provisional finding relevant to C-002:** centralised authority and task
standardisation reduced project delays associated with IT-workforce turnover,
especially voluntary and managerial-position turnover. The author interprets
these structures as retention facilities for task knowledge and the result as a
contingent turnover–performance relationship.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Theorised and structurally proxied | Task knowledge is proposed to persist in authority and standardisation; prior reasoning itself is not observed in the abstract. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Not directly measured | Workforce turnover is treated as a knowledge-loss risk, but retrieval or application failure is not an observed outcome. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Not measured | Project delay is measured; the work producing delay is not identified as reconstruction. |

**Alternative explanations:** authority may improve escalation or coordination;
standardisation may reduce task variability; turnover may disrupt capacity,
staffing or project continuity independently of memory; and agency or project
characteristics may shape both structure and delay.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** the outcome is delay rather than reconstruction;
the memory interpretation depends on structural proxies; the abstract cannot
establish causal identification or measurement fidelity; and the federal IT
setting may not generalise to other organisations or work.

**Research Lead direction:** provisionally `Contextual`. The study supplies a
structural-buffering and contingency account but does not observe unavailable
reasoning or reconstruction.

## E-009 — Knowledge and Performance in Knowledge-Worker Teams

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** publisher version of record accessed through an
[author-uploaded full text](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/220534393_Knowledge_and_Performance_in_Knowledge-Worker_Teams_A_Longitudinal_Study_of_Transactive_Memory_Systems).
The publisher DOI is https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1040.0257.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation, final sample and broad longitudinal
description are accurate. The usable sample was 261 second-year MBA students in
64 consulting teams, reduced from 346 invitees in 71 teams after nonresponse and
a minimum-response rule. Teams completed 13-week consulting projects for real
client organisations over two annual cohorts. Membership was fixed once teams
were assigned.

**Method:** longitudinal team-level field study with surveys before work, after
a five-week planning phase and after an approximately eight-week implementation
phase. Transactive memory systems (TMSs) were measured twice with a 15-item
scale covering specialised expertise, credibility and coordination. The study
used hierarchical moderated regressions for antecedents and two-stage
least-squares regressions for performance and viability relationships. Outcomes
included team- and client-rated performance and member-rated viability.

**Finding relevant to C-002:** distributed expertise predicted planning-phase
TMS, with prior familiarity strengthening that relationship. Face-to-face
communication contributed to TMS development, while non-face-to-face
communication had contingent effects. Implementation-phase TMS was positively
related to client-rated performance, team-rated performance and viability. The
paper studies how teams locate and integrate members’ current expertise; it does
not study loss of previously available organisational reasoning.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Not established | Members possess distributed current expertise, but the study does not identify prior organisational reasoning that later becomes unavailable. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Adjacent mechanism only | The TMS scale and communication analysis concern knowing who knows what, credibility and coordination; no loss or failed retrieval event is observed. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Not measured | Performance, viability and a reversed “backtrack and start over” scale item are not an operational measure of reconstructing prior reasoning. |

**Alternative explanations:** team composition, familiarity, communication
mode, task demand, coordination, credibility and current expertise utilisation
can explain performance without memory loss or reconstruction.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** team assignments were not randomised; many
predictors and outcomes were self-reported; the sample comprised temporary MBA
teams rather than established organisational teams; the analysis observes one
project without turnover; and the study does not identify reasoning artifacts,
later unavailability or reconstruction effort.

**Research Lead direction:** `Contextual`. TMS is an established expertise-
location and integration mechanism relevant to OME’s boundaries, but this study
does not test C-002.
