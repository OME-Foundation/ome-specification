## E-004 — Remembrance of Things Past?

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** publisher version of record accessed through an
[author-uploaded full text](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/220534766_The_Remembrance_of_Things_Past_The_Dynamics_of_Organizational_Forgetting).
The publisher DOI is https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.1040.0273.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation is accurate. The registered population
and method were directionally correct but underspecified. The fieldwork covered
seven Cuban hotels between 1995 and 1999: five hotels in two international
strategic alliances and two Cuban-owned baseline cases. The authors report 78
interviews, averaging about 90 minutes, and 16 formal site visits. Interviews
covered management personnel, front-line employees, customers, parent-company
participants and government participants. Interview material was supplemented
with documentary and observational material.

**Method:** exploratory multiple-case study for theory development. The authors
constructed and repeatedly updated cases, used within-case and cross-case
analysis, and derived a two-dimensional typology: accidental versus purposeful
loss crossed with new versus established knowledge. This produces four modes:
dissipation, degradation, suspension and purging.

**Finding relevant to C-002:** the cases describe new practices that disappeared
without continuing managerial reinforcement and established capabilities whose
performance degraded. The paper interprets these observations as failures to
consolidate or maintain organisational knowledge. It also reports purposeful
forgetting and treats forgetting as beneficial or harmful depending on what is
lost and the surrounding context.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Partial | Some practices affected collective action or were embedded; other “new knowledge” never consolidated into organisational memory. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Partial | Cases describe routines no longer remaining active and performance degradation, but do not isolate reasoning retrieval. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Not measured | Repeated managerial correction and reinforcement are observed, but reconstruction of prior reasoning and its effort are not operationalised. |

**Alternative explanations:** turnover, weak consolidation, inadequate
maintenance, dependence on managerial supervision, unfamiliarity with imported
practices, cultural frames and power all appear within the authors’ account.
They should not be collapsed into retrieval failure.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** the sites are a theoretically selected,
hospitality-specific set in 1990s Cuba; the study develops theory rather than
estimating prevalence or causal effects; forgetting categories are
interpretations of qualitative observations; and no reconstruction-work outcome
is defined or measured.

**Research Lead direction:** `Contextual`. The paper supplies established
forgetting mechanisms and boundary conditions, but not the complete C-002
chain.

## E-005 — In Praise of Organizational Forgetting

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** version of record accessed through the
[Lancaster E-Prints copy preserved by CORE](https://core.ac.uk/download/pdf/9661042.pdf).
The publisher DOI is https://doi.org/10.1177/1056492611408508.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation, conceptual source type and three
perspectives are accurate. Calling the method a “conceptual review” is
appropriate; it is not a systematic review and reports no new empirical study.

**Method:** argumentative synthesis of organisational-learning literature using
cognitive, behavioural and social perspectives. The authors distinguish
unlearning as deliberate disposal of unwanted knowledge from forgetting as
knowledge loss that is not necessarily planned.

**Finding relevant to C-002:** the paper argues that forgetting can enable
innovation, adaptation, renewal and release from outdated practices, while also
causing loss of competence, accountability, networks, identity and lessons from
past error. It explicitly rejects treating forgetting as uniformly beneficial or
harmful and identifies unresolved empirical questions about retrieval,
performance and the circumstances in which knowledge should be retained.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Discussed conceptually | Examples include lost technical knowledge, rationale, routines and relationships. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Discussed conceptually | Access barriers, turnover, record decay and loss of rationale are described, not empirically tested by this paper. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Not measured | The article does not observe or quantify reconstruction work. |

**Alternative explanations:** transfer stickiness, physical or technical access
constraints, turnover, political suppression, deliberate unlearning and renewal
are treated as distinct processes or perspectives.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** claims about benefits and harms are conceptual
and illustrative; examples are not a sampled empirical population; no causal
comparison, prevalence estimate or reconstruction measure is supplied; and the
authors themselves call for further research on impact and retrieval.

**Research Lead direction:** `Contextual`. The source is an important
counterposition and scope boundary, not empirical evidence supporting or
conflicting with C-002.

## E-006 — Organizational and Individual Learning and Forgetting

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** incomplete. The publisher record is protected by an access
challenge, JSTOR provides a first-page preview, the former Cornell repository
URL returns `404`, and the CiteSeerX indexed copy did not deliver a valid PDF in
this review environment. Publisher metadata, the indexed article text and an
[authoritative Federal Reserve research summary](https://www.minneapolisfed.org/article/2011/is-forgetting-a-problem)
were inspected. This is not represented as a completed full-text appraisal.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation and contextual direction remain
accurate. The registered population and method were too general. Available
source material identifies monthly aircraft-production data for the McDonnell
Douglas MD-80 and Lockheed L-1011, including MD-80 labour-hour assembly costs
and production conditions. The analysis compares learning-curve or forgetting
interpretations with models incorporating production-rate changes, parts
shortages, strikes and grounded-aircraft demands. The indexed MD-80 regression
covers 1984–1987 with 55 monthly observations in the simpler specification.

**Provisional finding relevant to C-002:** small cost degradation around strikes
was followed by rapid recovery. The authors report no evidence of
organisational forgetting in either aircraft line and find that a more fully
specified production model gives a much smaller role to forgetting. For the
MD-80, production-rate changes, parts shortages, strikes and grounded-aircraft
demands explain cost movements that a simpler learning curve could label
forgetting.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Proxied | Accumulated production experience is treated as retained capability, not recorded reasoning. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Not measured | Productivity or cost movement is the outcome; retrieval and application of reasoning are not observed. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Not measured | The study does not observe work to reconstruct reasoning. |

**Alternative explanations:** production-rate change, out-of-sequence work,
parts shortages, strike effects and demands for parts from unfinished aircraft
are the study’s central alternatives to forgetting.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** production cost is an invalid direct proxy for
reasoning availability or reconstruction; the setting is two aircraft lines;
model specification and proprietary inside-firm data constrain replication; and
the Research Lead has not completed a page-by-page full-text check.

**Research Lead direction:** provisionally `Contextual`. The source warns
against an invalid proxy and supports competing production explanations, but it
does not test C-002. Direction must not be finalised from this partial appraisal.
