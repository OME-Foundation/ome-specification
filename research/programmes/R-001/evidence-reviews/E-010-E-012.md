## E-010 — The Supportive Effect of Patterns in Architecture Decision Recovery

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** publisher version inspected through the University of
Groningen repository record and an indexed full-text copy. The publisher DOI is
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.scico.2011.11.008.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation, controlled-experiment classification
and direction are accurate. Thirty-three software-engineering participants from
industry and academia took part across EuroPLoP 2009 and a 2011 industrial
software-architecture workshop. Participants recovered architectural decisions
from documentation of the JBoss J2EE application server. Approximately half
were instructed to focus on identifying architectural patterns; the remainder
used their experience and intuition.

**Method:** controlled experiment comparing the quality and quantity of
after-the-fact architectural decisions recovered by a pattern-focused treatment
and an unaided control approach. The paper distinguishes decision recovery—what
was decided and why—from reconstruction of the system’s structural architecture.

**Finding relevant to C-002:** focusing on patterns significantly improved the
quality of recovered decisions. The experiment did not provide conclusive
evidence that it increased the quantity recovered. The recovery task is direct
evidence that participants can reconstruct architectural decisions and aspects
of rationale from surviving artifacts. Statements that recovery is expensive,
however, motivate the study rather than report a measured organisational
consequence.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Partly designed into the task | The target decisions and rationale existed in the reference architecture, but the experiment does not observe their original availability within a participant’s organisation. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Simulated or task-imposed | Participants recover decisions from supplied documentation; original-architect absence and incomplete recall motivate the task but are not participant-level measured exposures. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Recovery observed; consequence not measured | Decision reconstruction quality and quantity are measured, but organisational cost, delay, rework or operational consequence is not. |

**Alternative explanations:** pattern knowledge can act as a search scaffold or
answer template; differences may reflect participant pattern expertise,
documentation cues or scoring alignment rather than recovery of otherwise lost
organisational memory.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** the recovery task uses one documented open-source
application in an experimental setting; participants are not the successor team
of an observed organisation; quality and quantity do not establish material
cost; the experiment evaluates a recovery technique rather than prevalence or
consequence; and rationale reconstructed against a reference may not equal the
original decision-makers’ reasoning.

**Research Lead direction:** `Contextual`. This is the clearest direct recovery
study in the register, but it still does not establish the consequential third
component of C-002.

## E-011 — Recovering Architectural Assumptions

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** publisher version accessed through the
[Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam author repository](https://www.cs.vu.nl/~hans/publications/y2006/RAAM/JSS-RAAM.pdf).
The publisher DOI is https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jss.2005.10.017.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation, method-paper classification and
direction are accurate but underspecified. The paper develops RAAM, the
Recovering Architectural Assumptions Method, and applies it extensively to
SalesPlus, a commercial Java/J2EE product for e-commerce channels. The case
reconstructs technical, organisational and managerial assumptions and their
relationships from product and development evidence.

**Method:** method development plus a single industrial case. RAAM combines
free and guided interviews with available financial reports, documentation,
version-control history, source-code metrics and source inspection. These inputs
identify suspicious effects that guide interviews and produce a modelled list of
assumptions. The method assumes access to code, development history and people
with system knowledge.

**Finding relevant to C-002:** the case shows that undocumented assumptions and
their architectural relationships can be recovered and used to assess change
scenarios. It also documents practical recovery difficulties: locating experts,
changing responsibilities, knowledge loss when original authors leave, the need
for deep system understanding, and the inability to recover every assumption.
Some recovered assumptions explain costly development or maintenance choices,
but those examples are retrospective case interpretations, not a measurement of
the organisational cost of performing recovery.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Partial | Assumptions influenced earlier architectural decisions, although some were always implicit rather than explicit organisational reasoning. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Partial | The case reports hidden relationships, changed responsibility and lost details; recovery still depends heavily on knowledgeable people and artifacts. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Reconstruction observed; consequence not measured | RAAM reconstructs assumptions and informs change-cost assessment, but recovery effort, delay and organisational consequence are not quantified or compared. |

**Alternative explanations:** implicit assumptions may never have been shared
organisational knowledge; difficulty may arise from system complexity, weak
traceability, unclear ownership or poor documentation rather than later memory
loss; and recovered assumptions may be analyst interpretations.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** one commercial product and one tailored method;
no control or comparator; recovery completeness is unknowable; knowledgeable
interviewees are required; cost examples concern changing the system rather than
the cost caused by unavailable reasoning; and the paper does not estimate
prevalence or net organisational impact.

**Research Lead direction:** `Contextual`. The paper directly demonstrates a
reconstruction method and several loss mechanisms but does not establish
consequential reconstruction cost.

## E-012 — Lack of Shared Understanding of Non-Functional Requirements

### Research Lead Source Appraisal

**Full-text access:** complete IEEE conference paper accessed through the
[IEEE Computer Society proceedings](https://conferences.computer.org/re/pdfs/RE2020-5WQ8UqP6D8uiEHLBag4wuX/743800a090/743800a090.pdf).
The publisher DOI is https://doi.org/10.1109/RE48521.2020.00021.

**Registration fidelity:** the citation, three-organisation setting, 41-task
sample, mixed-method design and contextual direction are accurate. The cases are
three small agile organisations using cloud platforms and scaling continuous
software engineering. Researchers and organisation members validated candidate
tasks and confirmed 41 as non-functional-requirements-related rework associated
with lack of shared understanding.

**Method:** collaborative multiple-case study combining organisational context,
task-management data, interviews or preliminary immersive work, and two
three-hour focus groups per organisation. For every confirmed task, a developer,
manager and two researchers discussed original work, rework, associated NFRs,
causes, avoidability, complexity and importance. Qualitative themes were member-
checked and triangulated with task data.

**Finding relevant to C-002:** participants classified 78% of the 41 tasks as
accidental or avoidable lack of shared understanding and 22% as essential to
learning and feedback. Contributing factors included rapid change, missing
domain knowledge and inadequate communication. The study therefore observes
real rework and a consequential work outcome, while retaining a beneficial-
learning boundary. It does not show that the missing understanding had first
been available as organisational reasoning and was later lost.

**C-002 component check:**

| Component | Coverage | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prior organisational availability | Not established | Original work existed, but shared understanding or rationale may never have existed across the relevant people. |
| Later inability to retrieve or apply | Not established | The study investigates lack of shared understanding, not a transition from available reasoning to failed retrieval. |
| Consequential reconstruction | Adjacent consequential work observed | Rework tasks are traced and classified, but they may correct misunderstanding, discover unknowns or revise implementation rather than reconstruct prior reasoning. |

**Alternative explanations:** rapid learning, unknown unknowns, initial
requirements ambiguity, domain-knowledge gaps, communication failure,
deprioritisation of NFRs, technical debt and cross-functional coordination can
produce rework without organisational forgetting.

**C-002-relevant limitations:** purposive local contacts and three small cases;
retrospective participant classification of rework and avoidability; no effort
or financial magnitude reported for the 41 tasks; shared understanding is
inferred through rework; causal direction can be reciprocal; and the full C-002
temporal chain is absent.

**Research Lead direction:** `Contextual`. The paper supplies the strongest
consequential-work observation in the batch, but not evidence that reconstruction
was caused by later unavailability of previously held reasoning.
