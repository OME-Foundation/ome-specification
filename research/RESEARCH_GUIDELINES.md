# Research conduct guidelines

Status: research governance; not accepted OME canon.

These guidelines describe how the OME programme behaves while creating and
assessing knowledge. They complement the record structures and decision rules
in the [research operating system](operating-system/README.md); they are not a
universal research methodology.

## Conduct principles

### 1. Reality outranks the programme

Evidence does not owe OME a favourable result. When observations conflict with
a theory, term, architecture or founder preference, change or reject the claim.
Do not reinterpret the evidence merely to preserve the programme.

### 2. Steelman alternatives

Represent the strongest credible competing explanation in terms its proponents
would recognise. Compare OME claims with implemented practices and mature
theories, not weak summaries or isolated failures.

### 3. Search for disconfirmation early

Every protocol states what would narrow, revise or reject its material claims.
Search deliberately for null findings, counterexamples, beneficial absence or
forgetting, existing integrated solutions and explanations based on adoption,
incentives or context.

### 4. Separate source, observation and interpretation

Record what a source reports or what was observed before recording OME's
interpretation. Do not convert inference into observation through confident
language. External evidence and programme-generated observations may receive
different record types later; until then, identify the source type explicitly.

### 5. Preserve negative and rejected results

Negative, inconclusive and contradictory findings are programme outputs.
Rejected claims and excluded sources retain stable records, reasons and history
so they are not silently rediscovered or selectively forgotten.

### 6. Give every claim a scope

State the unit of analysis, population, context, mechanism and outcome. A claim
without boundaries cannot be meaningfully supported, challenged or replicated.
Do not generalise beyond the populations and settings represented by evidence.

### 7. Define the decision before collecting evidence

A research item names the decision it enables and the observations that would
change that decision. Register protocol changes and deviations; do not rewrite
the question after seeing results without preserving the original.

### 8. Assess relevance and method, not prestige

A famous paper can be indirect evidence. A case study can be highly relevant
but methodologically limited. Evaluate provenance, directness, methodological
fitness, independence, bias, precision and applicability separately.

### 9. Do not count citations as confidence

Repeated citations may share one dataset, method or intellectual origin.
Confidence comes from the quality, relevance and independence of evidence and
from the treatment of alternatives—not a vote among documents.

### 10. Never create terminology without comparison

Before proposing a term, document the closest established constructs, their
scope and why they are inadequate. Prefer an existing term whenever it explains
the phenomenon sufficiently. Novelty requires added explanatory or practical
power, not memorability.

### 11. Make uncertainty and change visible

Use the claim, evidence and research lifecycles. Record confidence judgements,
counter-evidence, corrections, protocol deviations and classification changes.
Do not delete inconvenient history.

### 12. Protect people and boundaries

Do not collect interviews, workplace observations, customer information or
sensitive organisational records without an appropriate ethical, privacy and
disclosure plan. Private evidence cannot be presented as independently
verifiable public proof.

### 13. Prefer reproducible process

Preserve search dates, search strings, sources searched, selection criteria,
exclusion reasons, extraction fields, analysis decisions and usable artifacts.
Another competent team should be able to understand what was done and attempt
the same investigation.

### 14. Declare conflicts and role overlap

Record intellectual, financial, product and authorship interests. When one
person or agent performs multiple roles, make the overlap visible and require
an accountable independent review for material claim decisions.

## Protocol-change rule

Necessary protocol changes are permitted. Each change records:

1. the original protocol;
2. what changed and when;
3. why the change was necessary;
4. whether evidence already observed could have influenced it; and
5. the likely effect on bias, scope and interpretation.

Exploratory findings discovered after a change remain valuable but must not be
reported as if they were pre-specified tests.

## Foundations and adaptations

These guidelines adapt rather than claim to replace established research
integrity and evidence-synthesis guidance:

- ALLEA, *The European Code of Conduct for Research Integrity*, revised edition
  2023. https://allea.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/European-Code-of-Conduct-Revised-Edition-2023.pdf
- National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine,
  *Reproducibility and Replicability in Science* (Washington, DC: National
  Academies Press, 2019). https://doi.org/10.17226/25303
- Matthew J. Page et al., “The PRISMA 2020 Statement: An Updated Guideline for
  Reporting Systematic Reviews,” *BMJ* 372 (2021): n71.
  https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.n71
- Julian P. T. Higgins et al., eds., *Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews
  of Interventions*, version 6.5 (Cochrane, 2024).
  https://www.cochrane.org/authors/handbooks-and-manuals/handbook

PRISMA and Cochrane address systematic reviews, especially health evidence.
OME adopts their commitments to pre-specification, transparent selection,
bias assessment and cautious interpretation where applicable; it does not claim
that every OME investigation is a clinical-style systematic review.
