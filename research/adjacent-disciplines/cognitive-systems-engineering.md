# Cognitive systems engineering

## Established definition and frameworks

### Source findings

Cognitive systems engineering (CSE) studies and designs joint human-machine
systems for complex work. It treats cognition as distributed across people,
tools, representations, procedures, and environments rather than located only
inside an individual. Cognitive work analysis, ecological interface design,
joint cognitive systems, resilience engineering, and common-ground research
all address how people coordinate, adapt, detect anomalies, and maintain control
under uncertainty.

### OME interpretation

OME should adopt the joint-system unit of analysis. A repository or AI assistant
cannot be evaluated independently of users, incentives, workflows, authority,
and operational conditions. “Human in the loop” is too weak if the human lacks
time, evidence, competence, or power to challenge the system.

Claims that AI can reconstruct organisational truth or reduce memory failure
must be evaluated as changes to a socio-technical control system, including new
failure modes such as automation bias, brittle representations, and hidden work.

## Problems handled well

- Designing for complex, adaptive, safety-relevant work.
- Analysing distributed cognition and coordination.
- Understanding work-as-done rather than only work-as-imagined.
- Designing representations that support anomaly detection and control.

## Limitations relevant to OME

CSE does not supply a universal organisational-memory schema or cross-lifecycle
provenance model. Its analyses are often domain-intensive and resist simplistic
standardisation—which is a warning for OME metrics, not a defect.
