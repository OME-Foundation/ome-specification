# Enterprise architecture

## Established definition and frameworks

### Source findings

Enterprise architecture (EA) describes and governs the relationships among an
organisation's strategy, capabilities, processes, information, applications,
and technology. TOGAF provides an Architecture Development Method, content and
governance concepts, and configurable guidance. ArchiMate provides a modelling
language across business, application, and technology layers. ISO/IEC/IEEE
42010 standardises concepts for architecture descriptions, stakeholders,
concerns, viewpoints, views, and model kinds.

### OME interpretation

OME should not recreate capability maps, value streams, viewpoints, architecture
principles, roadmaps, repositories, or governance. It should integrate with EA
and ask a narrower question: how are the evidence and reasoning behind
enterprise change preserved, challenged, traced, and learned from over time?

EA also demonstrates that comprehensive frameworks can become adoption-heavy.
OME should define a small conceptual core and profiles for use contexts rather
than one mandatory enterprise metamodel.

## Problems handled well

- Aligning change with strategy and capabilities.
- Providing stakeholder-specific views of complex systems.
- Governing architecture evolution and portfolios.
- Modelling dependencies and transition states.

## Limitations relevant to OME

EA artefacts may record approved target states without preserving the full
discovery history, rejected alternatives, evidence, or operational learning.
This varies by practice; it is not an inherent absence from EA.

## OME overlap and possible gap

| Founding idea | Assessment |
| --- | --- |
| Map objectives to implementation | **Established** in EA and traceability. |
| Architecture decisions need rationale | **Established**. |
| Preserve reasoning across enterprise change | **Synthesis**. |
| OME maturity model | **Hypothesis/Synthesis**; must avoid duplicating EA/KM maturity models. |
